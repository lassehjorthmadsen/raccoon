Raccoon Training Analysis

AlphaZero self-play for backgammon

Setup

Experiment Overview

This analysis covers all Raccoon training experiments, comparing their progress, efficiency, and performance. Each experiment tests a different training configuration to understand what drives learning in AlphaZero-style self-play.

| Experiment                     | Network      |   MCTS Sims |   Games/Iter |   Iterations |   Total Games | Status   |
|:-------------------------------|:-------------|------------:|-------------:|-------------:|--------------:|:---------|
| exp000-6x128-25sims            | 6x128 ResNet |          25 |           10 |          499 |          4990 | Complete |
| exp001-6x128-200sims           | 6x128 ResNet |         200 |           50 |          283 |         14150 | Complete |
| exp002-6x128-200sims-50kbuf    | 6x128 ResNet |         200 |           50 |          148 |          7400 | Complete |
| exp003-6x128-100sims-dirichlet | 6x128 ResNet |         100 |           25 |          138 |          3450 | Complete |
| exp004-6x128-100sims-bootstrap | 6x128 ResNet |         100 |           25 |          264 |          6600 | Complete |

Key Questions:

  1. exp000 (validation run): Does the full pipeline work? (M7 encoder, GPU training, spot VM recovery)
  2. exp001 (Phase 1): Does more compute per iteration (8x more MCTS simulations, 5x more games) translate to faster learning?
  3. exp002 (smaller replay buffer): Same config as exp001 but --replay-size 50000 instead of 500000. Tests whether replay-buffer staleness was contributing to the rising value loss observed in exp001 — a 50k buffer cycles in ~10 iterations vs ~100, so the network is always training on fresh self-play data.
  4. exp003 (Dirichlet noise + visit entropy tracking): Adds standard AlphaZero Dirichlet root noise (alpha=0.3, eps=0.25) and reduces the replay buffer further to 25k. Tests whether exploration collapse was causing the plateau. Abandoned at iter 284 with no evidence of improvement.
  5. exp004 (value bootstrapping + LR schedule): Blends MCTS root Q with the terminal game outcome as the value target (--value-bootstrap-alpha 0.5), adds Dirichlet noise, and applies a step-decay LR schedule (×0.1 at iter 200). Tests whether noisy terminal-only value targets are the root cause of the plateau, and whether LR decay reduces late oscillation.
  6. Is training actually converging? Loss curves are one signal; gammon/backgammon rates, game length, and the within-experiment strength curve are more diagnostic. Several candidate explanations exist for the trends we see — this analysis tries to distinguish them.

Loss Curves Comparison

Comparing loss trajectories across experiments reveals how different training configurations affect learning dynamics.

Observations:

Policy vs value loss are doing different things — they need to be read separately.

exp000 (25 sims, 10 games):

  • Policy loss declined steadily from ~6.5 to ~1.77 over 499 iterations
  • Value loss drifts upward slowly (~0.097 early → ~0.112 late) — not stable, though the change is small in absolute terms
  • So the policy head is fitting the replay distribution, but the value head is fitting it slightly worse over time

exp001 (200 sims, 50 games, 500k buf):

  • Fresh start (no resume from exp000)
  • Policy loss drops early (~4.4 → ~2.0), then flattens / drifts up slightly
  • Value loss rises clearly: ~0.105 → ~0.140
  • Total loss increase is dominated by the value head

exp002 (200 sims, 50 games, 50k buf):

  • Fresh start (no resume), 148 iterations
  • Policy loss drops faster than exp001 (~6.1 → bottoms ~2.00 at iter_86 → drifts up to ~2.05 at iter_147)
  • Value loss bottoms lower than exp001 ever reached (~0.103 at iter_82) but also drifts up (~0.119 at iter_147)
  • The smaller buffer does what we expected on the fitting side: faster initial descent, lower minimum, less aggressive late drift. However, benchmarks confirm this did not translate to stronger play: 0-50 vs GNUBG world-class (-2.300 ppg, identical to exp001 iter_282), and exp001 iter_282 beats exp002 iter_147 head-to-head (30-20, -0.440 ppg)

exp003 (100 sims, 25 games, 25k buf, Dirichlet):

  • Resumed from an existing checkpoint at iter 148 with an empty replay buffer
  • Policy loss jumped from ~1.88 → ~2.19 in the first 15 iterations, then immediately plateaued. This is a buffer fill artifact: the buffer was empty at resume, so the first few iterations trained on a concentrated slice of the current policy (low cross-entropy); as it filled with ~25k diverse positions over ~11 iterations, the loss rose and locked in
  • Visit entropy tracked for the first time: flat at ~1.37 throughout all 137 logged iterations — no improvement in MCTS decisiveness despite Dirichlet noise. For context, a uniform distribution over typical backgammon move counts (~20–30 legal actions per decision) would give entropy ≈ log(25) ≈ 3.2 nats. At 1.37, MCTS visits are concentrated at roughly 43% of the theoretical maximum — already quite peaked. “Not collapsing” is the right summary, but the absolute level is low enough to raise the question of whether Dirichlet ε=0.25 is actually diversifying an already-peaked prior, or getting washed out by confident logits
  • Experiment abandoned at iter 284 with no evidence of improvement

exp004 (100 sims, 25 games, 25k buf, bootstrap α=0.5, LR decay):

  • Fresh start from iter 0 with the same Dirichlet noise settings as exp003
  • Policy loss: 6.04 → 2.22 over 264 iterations — normal descent from a random init
  • Value loss: 0.038 at iter 0, drifting slightly to 0.053 by iter 263. This is an order of magnitude lower than exp001/exp002 (0.105–0.140). The low value loss reflects smoother bootstrap targets (50% root Q blend), not necessarily better value estimation
  • Visit entropy started high at 2.33 (fresh random network) and declined to 1.42 by iter 263 — natural convergence from a random prior toward a peaked policy, the same endpoint seen in exp003 (which started from a trained checkpoint at 1.37 and stayed flat)
  • LR decayed from 0.001 to 0.0001 at iter 199; no stabilization of value loss was visible after the decay — the slight upward drift continued at the same rate

Candidate explanations (we don’t yet know which apply):

  1. Self-play non-stationarity. If the network is genuinely improving, the positions it generates get harder, so loss can rise while strength rises. (Documented in AlphaZero literature.)
  2. Replay buffer staleness. With 500k capacity and ~5k positions/iter, the buffer holds ~100 iterations of positions. The network is being asked to simultaneously fit old (weaker-play) and new (less-weak) positions, which may be inconsistent.
  3. Value target noise. Training labels are {±1/3, ±2/3, ±1} — discrete and extreme. If the value head’s residual error is dominated by label noise, loss can look flat-or-rising even when the head is learning.
  4. Actual regression or overfitting. Possible but less likely given policy loss trajectory.

The “Self-Play Non-Stationarity” section below discusses what evidence would distinguish these. Short version: a within-experiment strength comparison (iter_k vs iter_k-20 at equal eval sims) is the only clean test. This has now been run for both exp001 (iter_282 vs iter_260: +0.120 ppg, n.s.) and exp002 (iter_147 vs iter_125: +0.300 ppg, n.s.) — neither shows detectable strength gain, confirming the plateau.

Self-Play Non-Stationarity vs. Other Explanations

In supervised learning, rising loss means the model is getting worse. In self-play, rising loss may indicate the network is improving — but it may also indicate other things. Distinguishing them matters.

The non-stationarity story:

  • The network trains against itself
  • As the network improves, the opponent gets stronger
  • Stronger opponent → harder positions → higher loss
  • Loss measures “how well do I fit my current training data”, not “how strong am I at backgammon”

This is well-documented in AlphaZero papers and is genuinely possible here.

Competing explanations that are also consistent with our data:

  • Replay buffer mixing old and new games. exp001’s 500k buffer holds ~100 iterations of positions. If earlier play is qualitatively different from later play, the network is being asked to fit an inconsistent distribution.
  • Value target distribution. Labels are discrete {±1/3, ±2/3, ±1}. In practice ~half of self-play games end in gammon or backgammon, so labels sit at the extremes. The value head is calibrated for a distribution dominated by large-margin outcomes, which may not generalize to close positions against a stronger opponent.
  • Exploration collapse. If the MCTS policy concentrates visits on a few actions early, self-play stops surfacing novel positions and the distribution ossifies. Symptom: flat gammon/backgammon rates across iterations.

What would distinguish these:

  • Within-experiment strength curve. Arena match iter_k vs iter_k-20 at equal eval sims. If strength rises, non-stationarity fits; if flat, the network is plateaued regardless of what the loss does. Run for exp001 (iter_282 vs iter_260: +0.120 ppg, n.s.), exp002 (iter_147 vs iter_125: +0.300 ppg, n.s.), exp003 (iter_220 vs iter_150: -0.430 ppg — newer model lost), and exp004 (iter_260 vs iter_200: -0.100 ppg combined over 100 games, n.s.) — all confirm plateau or regression across every tested configuration.
  • Gammon/backgammon trend over training. If declining: play quality is improving and the non-stationarity story is plausible. If flat: play is stuck and rising loss is not masking improvement.
  • MCTS visit-entropy trend. Collapsing entropy → exploration is dying.

Evidence from the data we do have (see “Game Dynamics” below): gammon and backgammon rates are flat across all five experiments. That is evidence against the pure non-stationarity story, or at least evidence that whatever improvement is happening is too small to show up in game outcomes.

What about exp001 beating exp000? The arena match (32-18, +0.44 ppg) compared exp001 iter_112 vs exp000 iter_498. These differ in training config (8× sims, 5× games) AND checkpoint history. A win here only proves “more compute + more training + more sims at eval time beats less.” It does not show that exp001’s loss increase masks real strength gain, because it doesn’t hold exp001’s config fixed across checkpoints.

The within-experiment strength comparison has been run for exp001. iter_0282 vs iter_0260 (50 games, 100 eval sims): 27-23, +0.120 ppg. With n=50, the per-game equity SE is roughly 0.25 ppg, so this is not statistically distinguishable from zero (~+0.5z). Across 22 iterations of training, no detectable strength gain. Rising value loss is not masking a strength improvement that’s just too small to see in self-play game outcomes — it isn’t visible in head-to-head play between checkpoints either.

exp003 check shows regression, not just plateau. iter_220 vs iter_150 (100 games, 50 eval sims): 40-60, -0.430 ppg. The newer checkpoint actually lost to the older one. With Dirichlet noise and a fresh 25k buffer, the network did not improve from iter 150 to iter 220 — it got weaker.

exp004 shows the same pattern despite bootstrapping and LR decay. iter_260 vs iter_200 (100 games total, 100 eval sims): combined result 48-52, -0.100 ppg (two 50-game matches: +0.120 ppg then -0.320 ppg). Not statistically significant (SE ≈ 0.18 ppg over 100 games), but there is no detectable gain in the 60 iterations following the LR decay at iter 200. The bootstrapped value targets produced much lower MSE loss — but lower loss on smoother targets did not translate to stronger play.

Loss Trend Detail: Policy and Value Separately

Policy and value loss are doing different things; reading them together via total_loss obscures the signal. We fit a linear trend on each over a “late phase” (after the first 20% of each run), so the early warmup doesn’t dominate the slope.

Code 
from numpy.polynomial.polynomial import polyfit

n_exp = len(experiments)
fig, axes = plt.subplots(n_exp, 2, figsize=(14, 5 * n_exp), squeeze=False)

for col_idx, (col, label) in enumerate([
    ("policy_loss", "Policy Loss (cross-entropy)"),
    ("value_loss", "Value Loss (MSE)"),
]):
    for row_idx, (exp_id, exp) in enumerate(experiments.items()):
        df = exp["df"]
        ax = axes[row_idx, col_idx]

        # Drop the first 20% so warmup doesn't dominate the fit.
        cutoff = int(len(df) * 0.2)
        late = df.iloc[cutoff:].copy()

        ax.plot(late["iteration"], late[col], alpha=0.3, color=exp["color"], linewidth=0.8)
        ax.plot(late["iteration"], late[col].rolling(20, min_periods=1).mean(),
                color=exp["color"], linewidth=2.5, label=f"{label} (rolling mean)")

        x = late["iteration"].values
        y = late[col].values
        b, m = polyfit(x, y, 1)
        ax.plot(x, b + m * x, color="red", linestyle="--", linewidth=1.5,
                label=f"Slope: {m:+.5f}/iter")

        ax.set_xlabel("Iteration")
        ax.set_ylabel(label)
        ax.set_title(f"{exp['label']}: {label}", fontweight="bold", fontsize=10)
        ax.legend(fontsize=8)
        ax.grid(True, alpha=0.3)

        first_loss = late[col].rolling(20).mean().dropna().iloc[0] if len(late) >= 20 else late[col].iloc[0]
        last_loss = late[col].rolling(20).mean().iloc[-1]
        print(f"{exp['label']} — {label}: {first_loss:.4f} → {last_loss:.4f} "
              f"(Δ={last_loss-first_loss:+.4f}, slope={m:+.6f}/iter)")

fig.tight_layout()
plt.show()

Interpretation:

  • Policy loss measures how well the network predicts MCTS visit distributions. Declining slope → the network is tracking MCTS’s recommendations. Flat/rising slope → policy head has plateaued or is overfit against a drifting target.
  • Value loss measures MSE against discrete game outcomes in {±1/3, ±2/3, ±1}. A rising trend is more concerning than for policy loss, because the label distribution is bounded and doesn’t get harder in absolute terms — it shifts as self-play improves, but the variance ceiling is fixed.
  • A positive slope is not automatically “the opponent is stronger.” See alternative explanations above; the decisive test is a within-experiment strength comparison.

Game Dynamics: Is Play Quality Improving?

The clearest diagnostic for whether training is making progress isn’t loss — it’s whether the distribution of game outcomes is shifting. In strong-vs-strong backgammon, gammon rates sit around 15–20% and backgammons around 1%. If our self-play gammon rate is falling toward those numbers, play is improving. If it’s flat, play is stuck.

Observations (revised, blunt):

  • Game length is stable at ~80–95 moves across all runs. Not a strength signal either way — normal for any level of play.
  • Gammon rates are flat at 33–35% (exp000), 38–43% (exp001), ~40% (exp002), ~44% (exp003), ~39–42% (exp004) across all iterations. That is 2–3× the rate of strong-vs-strong play. If training were making the network materially stronger, this number should be trending down. It isn’t, in any of the five runs.
  • Backgammon rates vary across experiments: exp000 ~15%, exp001 ~19%, exp002 ~20%, exp003 ~14%, exp004 ~29%. Strong play produces ~1%. None are improving over training iterations. Note that exp003 and exp004 have only 25 games per iteration (vs 50 for exp001/002), so per-iteration rates are noisier; the aggregated counts are the more reliable measure.
  • These rates indicate weak play, not “intermediate” play. 50–65% of self-play games ending in gammon or backgammon is the fingerprint of players who leave checkers stranded and walk into large losses.
  • exp001’s gammon rate is slightly higher than exp000’s, despite 8× the MCTS sims. Possible explanation: with longer, denser self-play, the network spends more time in positions where one side is already losing, making completion-to-gammon more likely.
  • exp003 (100 sims, 25 games, 25k buf, Dirichlet): Gammon rate ~44%, backgammon rate ~14%. Visit entropy flat at ~1.37 throughout — Dirichlet noise kept exploration alive in principle but did not improve the policy prior enough to shift MCTS visit concentration or game outcomes. The pattern is indistinguishable from exp001/exp002.
  • exp004 (100 sims, 25 games, 25k buf, bootstrap α=0.5, LR decay): Gammon rate ~39%, indistinguishable from other runs. Backgammon rate ~29% across all iterations, rising to ~32% in the last 50 — notably higher than any other experiment, not lower. The bootstrapped value targets did not produce more cautious or competent play. Visit entropy declined from 2.33 (random init) to 1.42 — the same endpoint as other experiments, reached through natural policy sharpening rather than maintaining the initial diversity.
  • exp002’s smaller buffer (50k vs 500k) didn’t shift the gammon/bg distribution. The loss curves did improve (faster descent, lower minima), but game outcomes look the same as exp001. Benchmarks confirm: 0-50 vs GNUBG world-class (-2.300 ppg), virtually indistinguishable from exp001 iter_282’s -2.32 ppg.

Takeaway for the training plateau: combined with rising (or already-very-low bootstrapped) value loss, the flat gammon/backgammon rates across all five experiments are the strongest evidence that self-play is stuck in a weak-play equilibrium. The network fits the distribution of its own games, but that distribution isn’t shifting toward stronger play — not with smaller buffers, Dirichlet noise, bootstrapped targets, or LR decay.

Player-0 Equity Bias

OpenSpiel’s opening roll randomly assigns which player moves first (verified 50/50). Under symmetric self-play, the average game outcome from player 0’s perspective should be approximately zero.

Interpretation:

  • exp000 shows a persistent +0.3 bias toward player 0 across all 500 iterations. Random-vs-random play (verified with 3000 games) gives +0.04 ± 0.04 — statistically indistinguishable from zero. So the engine is symmetric, and the bias in exp000 is coming from the interaction of the weakly-trained network with shallow (25-sim) MCTS. A small asymmetry in the network’s raw outputs gets amplified by low-sim search and then reinforced by training on the resulting self-play games.
  • exp001’s bias is much smaller (~+0.08, marginally non-zero). With 200 sims, MCTS drowns out the network’s residual asymmetry — which is consistent with the “bias is a network artifact, not an engine bug” diagnosis.
  • This is not the main training problem, but it is a useful thermometer for whether search depth is adequate to correct the raw network.

Summary and Next Steps

Key Findings Across Experiments

1. Policy and value loss diverge — read them separately.

  • exp000: policy loss drops smoothly (6.5 → 1.77), value loss drifts up slowly (0.097 → 0.112).
  • exp001: policy loss drops early then flattens/drifts; value loss rises more clearly (0.105 → 0.140).
  • exp003: policy loss jumped from ~1.88 to ~2.19 in the first 15 iterations (buffer fill artifact at resume), then plateaued flat. Value loss flat at ~0.06. Neither head improved over 137 iterations.
  • exp004: policy loss dropped from 6.04 to 2.22 over 264 iterations (normal descent). Value loss: 0.038 → 0.053. This is an order of magnitude lower than exp001/exp002 — a direct effect of the bootstrapped value targets (50% root Q blend), which are smoother than raw terminal outcomes. Lower MSE on smoother targets does not imply a better value function; the test is play quality.
  • Rising value loss (exp001/002) is not by itself proof of improvement or regression. Very low value loss (exp004) is not proof of good value estimation. Both are signals about how well the value head fits its training targets — which has several candidate explanations tied to whether the targets are meaningful.

2. Self-play appears plateaued at a weak-play equilibrium across all five experiments.

  • Gammon rates are flat at ~33–44% across all runs. Strong-vs-strong play is ~15–20%.
  • Backgammon rates range from ~14% (exp003) to ~29% (exp004); all far above the ~1% of strong play and none declining over training iterations.
  • These rates not declining is the strongest evidence that the networks are not materially improving at the thing that matters (avoiding catastrophic positions).
  • Against GNUBG world-class, exp001 conceded 23 backgammons in 50 games (46%), consistent with this.
  • exp002’s smaller buffer didn’t move this needle — confirmed by benchmarks. exp002 iter_147 vs GNUBG world-class: 0-50, -2.300 ppg (virtually identical to exp001’s -2.32 ppg). Within-experiment: iter_147 vs iter_125, 26-24 (52%), +0.300 ppg (not significant, SE ≈ 0.25 ppg). Cross-experiment: exp001 iter_282 beats exp002 iter_147 (30-20, -0.440 ppg). The staleness fix improved fitting but not play quality; exp001 remains stronger despite worse loss curves.
  • exp003’s Dirichlet noise didn’t move this needle. Visit entropy stayed flat at ~1.37 for all 137 logged iterations. The plateau is not caused by exploration collapse alone; standard Dirichlet noise at this sim count and network strength did not improve the policy prior enough to shift game outcomes.
  • exp004’s value bootstrapping and LR decay didn’t move this needle either. Gammon rate ~39–42%, backgammon rate ~29–32% — indistinguishable from other experiments (if anything slightly worse). Lower value loss from smoother targets did not translate to better play.

3. No detectable within-experiment strength gain in any of the five experiments.

  • exp001: iter_282 vs iter_260, 27-23, +0.120 ppg (n=50, SE ≈ 0.25 ppg) — not significant.
  • exp002: iter_147 vs iter_125, 26-24, +0.300 ppg (n=50, SE ≈ 0.25 ppg) — not significant.
  • exp003: iter_220 vs iter_150, 40-60, -0.430 ppg (n=100) — outright regression. The newer checkpoint is weaker than the older one.
  • exp004: iter_260 vs iter_200, 48-52, -0.100 ppg combined (n=100, SE ≈ 0.18 ppg) — not significant, slight regression.
  • Combined with flat gammon/BG rates and GNUBG world-class 0-50 results, the training plateau is not masking a small strength improvement — it is a genuine plateau. Every variation tested (buffer size, Dirichlet noise, bootstrapping, LR decay) has produced the same pattern: no detectable strength gain within the run.

4. Player-0 equity bias is a training artifact of low-sim MCTS, not an engine bug.

  • exp000: +0.3 equity bias for player 0, persistent across all iterations.
  • exp001: reduced to ~+0.08 with deeper search.
  • Random-vs-random baseline (n=3000): +0.04 ± 0.04. Engine is symmetric.
  • A small asymmetry in the raw network outputs gets amplified by shallow MCTS and reinforced by training. More sims dilute it.

5. Training efficiency

  • exp001 is ~27× slower per iteration (37.5 min vs 1.4 min) due to 8× MCTS sims and 5× games.
  • SGD cost depends on hardware. On the T4 GPU (exp001, exp002): ~3 sec/iter — negligible. On CPU (exp003, exp004): ~500 sec and ~210 sec respectively. Self-play is always the dominant cost on GPU; on CPU, SGD becomes a significant fraction.
  • exp002’s 50k buffer improved fitting metrics but not play quality vs exp001’s 500k buffer. Buffer size is not the lever that breaks the plateau.
  • exp003 runs ~29 min/iter on CPU (100 sims, 25 games, including ~500 sec SGD). exp004 runs ~16 min/iter on CPU with bootstrapped targets (~210 sec SGD — cheaper than exp003’s pure-CE/MSE update).

6. All experiments are far from GNUBG-competitive.

  • exp000 iter_498: 0-10 vs GNUBG world-class (n=10, wide CI).
  • exp001 iter_112: 1-49 (-2.22 ppg) vs GNUBG world-class, 19 gammons and 22 backgammons conceded.
  • exp001 iter_282: 0-50 (-2.32 ppg) vs GNUBG world-class, 20 gammons and 23 backgammons conceded. After 170 more iterations, no improvement.
  • exp002 iter_147: 0-50 (-2.300 ppg) vs GNUBG world-class, 19 gammons and 23 backgammons conceded, 0 for Raccoon. Indistinguishable from exp001 despite better fitting metrics.
  • exp003, exp004: no GNUBG benchmark run.
  • M6 milestone (beat GNUBG at money game) is not in reach at current training settings.

Immediate Next Steps

exp003 and exp004 have both been completed. Five hypotheses have now been tested (buffer staleness, buffer size, exploration collapse via Dirichlet noise, value bootstrapping, LR decay) and none has broken the plateau. exp001 iter_282 remains the strongest checkpoint. The self-play signal itself is the bottleneck — no amount of tuning the replay buffer or value targets will fix a policy that trains on its own weak games.

  1. Run benchmarks for exp002. Done (2026-04-30). iter_147 vs iter_125: 26-24 (52%), +0.300 ppg (n.s.). vs GNUBG world-class: 0-50, -2.300 ppg. vs exp001 iter_282: 20-30, -0.440 ppg (exp001 wins). Conclusion: smaller buffer is not a play-quality improvement.

  2. Track MCTS visit-distribution entropy over training. Done (exp003). Visit entropy logged throughout; flat at ~1.37 for all 137 iterations. Exploration is not collapsing, but it is also not improving.

  3. Add Dirichlet root noise. Done (exp003). Dirichlet noise (alpha=0.3, eps=0.25) added in exp003. Did not break the plateau — iter_220 lost to iter_150 (-0.43 ppg) and iter_280 drew iter_150 (-0.13 ppg, n.s.). 130 iterations of training produced no detectable improvement. Standard root noise at this sim count was insufficient; exploration was not the sole root cause.

  4. Add a learning rate schedule. Done (exp004). LR decayed from 0.001 to 0.0001 at iter 200 (×0.1). Value loss continued its slight upward drift at the same rate after decay; no stabilization. Within-experiment strength at iter_260 vs iter_200: 48-52, -0.100 ppg (n.s.). LR decay did not break the plateau.

  5. Consider value target bootstrapping. Done (exp004). --value-bootstrap-alpha 0.5 (50% root Q, 50% terminal outcome). Value loss dropped to 0.038–0.053 (vs 0.105–0.140 in exp001/002), confirming smoother targets. Game quality unchanged: gammon rate ~39–42%, backgammon rate ~29–32%, no within-experiment strength gain. Lower MSE on bootstrapped targets does not translate to stronger play.

  6. Scale sims, not iterations. Going to 400–800 sims is a more promising next lever than further iteration count or target tuning.

  7. Consider a bigger algorithmic pivot. After five failed interventions, next candidates: afterstate value learning, TD-style warm start, or pretraining on GNUBG positions followed by self-play fine-tuning. These would change the quality of the self-play signal itself, not just how it is consumed.

  8. Infrastructure. Future experiments should be added to the EXPERIMENTS dict; download logs from GCS and re-render.
